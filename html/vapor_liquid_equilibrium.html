Vapor-Liquid Equilibrium

Information

General

We are interested in the systems in which vapor and liquid phase coexists under given physic conditions.

In case of a simple fluid (i.e. fluid consisting of one chemical substance) its physical phase is "uniquely determined" by conditions. It means that although simple fluid could be represented by two different coexisting phases, such representations don't form a subset of full dimension in phase space. For our analysis such representations are transient and excluded from consideration.

Contrary, in case of a compound fluid (i.e. fluid consisting of more than one chemical substance) there are some regions of full dimension in phases space where fluid could coexist in different states. Specific information for vapor-liquid decomposition in equilibrium is presented in this section.

Characterization

In order to describe vapor-liquid decomposition of the compound fluid we need to know total fractions of liquid and vapor and the decompositions of liquid and gaseous parts into simple fluids.

In what follows we describe compound fluid as composition of $N$ simple fluids with molar percent of $i$th component as $z_i, (i = 1, 2, \dots, N)$. We denote total fraction of gaseous part as $\beta$. Liquid part decomposition is given by molar percents denoted as $x_i$ and gaseous part is given by molar percents denoted as $y_i$.

Necessary conditions for equilibrium of two phases could be described by the following equations:

  1. Equality of pressures: \[ P^\text{L} = P^\text{V} \]

  2. Equality of temperatures: \[ T^\text{L} = T^\text{V} \]

  3. Equality of chemical potentials of each component in both phases: \[ \mu_i^\text{L} = \mu_i^\text{V},~~~~i = 1, \dots, N \]

Once we need to compute some physical properties of such a system in equilibrium, we need to specify some chemical potentials or their equivalent among different possibilities. After potentials are specified we somehow solve characterizing equations for $\beta, \bar{x}, \bar{y}$ and use derived characterization for computation of physical properties.

Fugacities

General

One of the useful option is to chose fugacity as the equivalent of chemical potential. Fugacity represents the "effective pressure" of element in composition.

Fugacity could be defined (see Haile and O'Connell (2005), section 4.3.2) as such a property which helps to match differential of chemical potential $\bar{G}_i$ along isothermal change in the following manner: \[ d\bar{G}_i = RT d \ln \mathbb{f}_i \]

Fugacity Coefficients and Ideal Gas

Since we know only differential, our definition of fugacity incomplete. We need to know some reference value. In case when we have a mixture with fractions given by molar percents $z_i$, which we treat as an ideal gas, we chose this reference value to be \[ \mathbb{f}^{ig}_i = z_i P \] so that "effective pressure" of component in ideal gas mixture depends only on its molar percent.

Fugacity coefficient is a ratio measure that indicates how the fugacity of a real substance deviates from that of an ideal gas. From the fugacity definition: \[ \bar{G}_i(T, P, \bar{z}) - \bar{G}^{ig}_i(T, P, \bar{z}) = RT \ln \frac{\mathbb{f}_i(T, P, \bar{z})}{\mathbb{f}^{ig}_i(T, P, \bar{z})} \] The ratio of fugacities on the rhs is called the fugacity coefficient $\phi_i$: \[ \phi_i = \frac{\mathbb{f}_i(T, P, \bar{z})}{\mathbb{f}^{ig}_i(T, P, \bar{z})} = \frac{\mathbb{f}_i(T, P, \bar{z})}{z_i P} \]

Fugacity Coefficients for Equation of State

Suppose we are provided with a general cubic equation of state given in the form: \[ P = \frac{RT}{V - b} - \frac{\Theta(T)}{V^2 + \delta V + \epsilon} \]

With introduced new parameters $A = \frac{\Theta(T) P}{R^2 T^2}$, $B = \frac{bP}{RT}$, $D = \frac{\delta P}{RT}$ and $E = \frac{\epsilon P^2}{R^2 T^2}$ our equation changes into more standard-looking equation for a compressibility coefficient $Z = \frac{PV}{RT}$: \[ Z^3 + (A - B + D -1) Z^2 + (E - BD - D - AB) Z - (B + 1)E = 0 \]

This equation could have either one or three real roots. If there is only one real root, it means that only one phase possible under such conditions. If it has three roots then the smallest corresponds to a liquid phase and the biggest corresponds to a vapor phase.

Once we pick up a value for the compressibility factor, the following formulas could be applied for a fugacity coefficient calculation in new coefficients.

  • If $D^2 \neq 4E$ then \[ \log \phi = \frac{A}{(D^2 - 4 E)^{1/2}} \ln \left\{ \frac{2Z + D - (D^2 - 4 E)^{1/2}}{2Z + D + (D^2 - 4 E)^{1/2}} \right\} - \ln\left\{Z - B\right\} - (1 - Z) \]

  • otherwise \[ \log \phi = -\frac{P}{RT} \frac{2}{2Z + D} - \ln\left\{Z - B\right\} - (1 - Z) \]

In old coefficients this formulas are:

  • If $\delta^2 \neq 4\epsilon$ then \[ \log \phi = \frac{\Theta(T)}{RT(\delta^2 - 4 \epsilon)^{1/2}} \ln \left\{ \frac{2V + \delta - (\delta^2 - 4 \epsilon)^{1/2}}{2V + \delta + (\delta^2 - 4 \epsilon)^{1/2}} \right\} - \ln\left\{Z(1 - b/V)\right\} - (1 - Z) \]

  • otherwise \[ \log \phi = -\frac{1}{V + \delta / 2} - \ln\left\{Z(1 - b/V)\right\} - (1 - Z) \]

Computations

Phase Iterative Phi-Phi Division

Under specified temperature and pressure, phase equilibrium condition could be described by the following set of non-linear equations:

  • Materiel balance equations: \[ \beta y_i + (1 - \beta) x_i = z_i,~~~~i=1,\dots, N \]

  • Equilibrium equations: \[ y_i \phi_i^\text{V} = x_i \phi_i^\text{L},~~~~i=1,\dots, N \]

  • Summation of mole fractions: \[ \sum_{i=1}^N (y_i - x_i) = 0 \]

According to Meichelsen (1982b), the following computational procedure could be applied.

  1. We set up an initial guess for new parameters $K_i = \frac{y_i}{x_i}$. One of the option for this guess is due to Wilson (see formula 6.13 in Pedersen and Christensen (2006)): \[ K_i = \frac{P_{ci}}{P} \exp\left\{5.373 (1 + \omega_i) \left(1 - \frac{T_{ci}}{T}\right)\right\} \]

  2. We solve following equation for the $\beta$ (vapor fraction): \[ \sum_{i = 1}^{N} \frac{(K_i - 1)z_i}{1 + \beta(K_i - 1)} = 0 \]

  3. We compute component molar percents as \[ x_i = \frac{z_i}{1 + \beta (K_i - 1)},~~~~i=1,\dots, N \\ y_i = \frac{K_i z_i}{1 + \beta (K_i - 1)},~~~~i=1,\dots, N \]

  4. Based on these molar percents we compute fugacity coefficients and substitute their ratio for $K_i$: \[ K_i \leftarrow \frac{\phi_i^\text{L}\left(\bar{x}\right)}{\phi_i^\text{V}\left(\bar{y}\right)} \] and then we start again with these new guesses.

Iterations should be repeated until required precision is achieved.
